\begin{abstract}
Large-scale electric vehicle (EV) charging networks require real-time coordination under uncertain vehicle arrivals, heterogeneous departure deadlines, time-varying electricity prices, and transformer capacity limits. Offline centralized optimization can obtain low charging cost when complete future information is available, but it is not directly causal. This paper develops an online risk-aware distributed EV charging framework based on deadline-aware virtual queues, Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty control, and ADMM-based per-slot coordination. The remaining charging demand of each EV is modeled as a dynamic queue, and a risk buffer protects the transformer capacity constraint against base-load prediction errors. Numerical experiments compare uncontrolled charging, greedy charging, offline centralized optimization, dual decomposition, offline ADMM, and the proposed online Lyapunov-ADMM method. In synthetic tight-capacity cases, the proposed method improves peak-load and capacity-safety metrics at the cost of higher charging cost than offline benchmarks. A public-data check indicates that real office-parking sessions require joint tuning of cost, deadline, and risk parameters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Coordinated EV charging is becoming an important problem in smart grids because uncoordinated charging can increase electricity cost, create new peak loads, stress distribution transformers, and complicate vehicle-grid interaction \cite{Clement2010,Deilami2011,Guille2009,Richardson2013,Yilmaz2012}. The scheduling problem is challenging because EVs have heterogeneous arrival times, departure deadlines, energy demands, and charging-rate limits. In addition, electricity prices and non-EV base load vary over time, so a charging policy should balance cost saving, peak-load reduction, user satisfaction, and transformer safety.

Many EV charging studies formulate the problem as an offline optimization task, where all vehicle information is known before scheduling. Such formulations are useful benchmarks, but practical charging stations often operate online: vehicles arrive randomly, remaining demand changes over time, and the aggregator must decide before future arrivals are known. The central question is therefore how an EV charging network can make scalable and risk-aware online decisions while preserving deadline satisfaction.

The method combines Lyapunov online control with ADMM-based distributed coordination. Lyapunov control converts long-term deadline pressure into a sequence of tractable per-slot optimization problems. ADMM decomposes each per-slot problem into EV-side local updates and aggregator-side coordination. This design keeps the controller causal while retaining the distributed structure emphasized in decomposition-based EV charging methods.

The contributions are summarized as follows. First, an online EV charging model is formulated with deadline-aware virtual queues, time-varying prices, and risk-buffered transformer constraints. Second, a Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty objective is derived to trade off charging cost, peak-load smoothing, and deadline urgency. Third, an ADMM-based distributed solver is implemented and compared with centralized optimization, dual decomposition, offline ADMM, greedy charging, and uncontrolled charging.

\section{Related Work}
Centralized EV charging optimization has been widely studied for valley filling, cost minimization, grid constraints, and regulation services. Sortomme and El-Sharkawi developed optimal charging strategies for unidirectional vehicle-to-grid services \cite{Sortomme2011}. Sundstrom and Binding considered flexible charging with distribution-grid constraints \cite{Sundstrom2012}. Charging and discharging schedules, battery-health-aware charging, renewable-aware charging, and real-time charging-park management have also been studied in broader EV-smart-grid settings \cite{He2012,Bashash2010,Mwasilu2014,Zakariazadeh2014}. These methods provide useful benchmarks, but they typically require a central aggregator and sufficient information about the charging population.

Decentralized and distributed EV charging control has also received significant attention. Gan, Topcu, and Low proposed decentralized and schedule-constrained charging protocols that exploit EV flexibility for valley filling \cite{Gan2013,Gan2013Allerton}. Ma, Callaway, and Hiskens studied decentralized charging control for large plug-in EV populations using game-theoretic ideas \cite{Ma2013}. Other online or heuristic methods address practical charging constraints and discrete charging rates \cite{Alonso2014,Masoum2015,Lee2021Discrete}. These works show that distributed coordination can reduce central computation and communication burden.

ADMM is a standard tool for distributed convex optimization. Boyd et al. provide a general treatment of ADMM and explain why it is well suited to decomposable large-scale optimization problems \cite{Boyd2011}. The method is connected to Douglas--Rachford splitting, augmented Lagrangian decomposition, proximal algorithms, and multi-agent optimization \cite{Eckstein1992,Gabay1976,Parikh2014,Nedic2009}. Related ADMM ideas have been used in distributed optimal power flow \cite{DallAnese2013,Erseghe2014}. In EV charging, ADMM-style methods are attractive because EV-level objectives are local while transformer-capacity constraints are globally coupled.

Online stochastic optimization is another relevant line of work. Queue-based MaxWeight ideas provide the foundation for throughput-aware online scheduling \cite{Tassiulas1992,Tassiulas1993,Georgiadis2006,Stolyar2004}. Neely's Lyapunov optimization framework shows how drift-plus-penalty methods can make online decisions in stochastic systems without requiring future information \cite{Neely2010}. Risk-aware capacity management is related to robust, stochastic, and chance-constrained optimization \cite{Bertsimas2004,Bertsimas2011,BenTal2009,Shapiro2014,Nemirovski2006,Calafiore2006,Campi2008}. Reinforcement learning provides another online-control direction \cite{Sutton2018,Watkins1992,Mnih2015}; this paper instead uses a convex queue-based controller for interpretability and tunability.

Empirical charging datasets are increasingly important for validating charging algorithms. ACN-Data provides an open workplace and public charging dataset \cite{Lee2019}. Recent office-building charging studies analyze information value, predictive charging, parking-lot allocation, and user-facing guarantees \cite{Winschermann2023Value,Hoogsteen2023Robust,Arias2023Parking,Winschermann2023Guarantees}. This study uses synthetic experiments for controlled sweeps and performs a public-data check with the 4TU/ElaadNL large office parking-lot dataset \cite{DeBont2025ElaadOffice}. The contribution is to integrate deadline-aware Lyapunov queues with ADMM-based per-slot coordination and to evaluate cost, peak load, safety, scalability, and public-session sensitivity in a common framework.

\section{System Model}
Time is divided into $T$ slots of length $\Delta t$. Let $\mathcal{N}(t)$ be the set of active EVs at slot $t$. Each EV $i$ has arrival time $a_i$, departure time $d_i$, energy demand $E_i$, maximum charging power $p_i^{\max}$, and charging efficiency $\eta_i$. The decision variable $p_i(t)$ denotes the charging power assigned to EV $i$ at slot $t$.

The basic constraints are
\begin{align}
0 \le p_i(t) \le p_i^{\max}, \quad i \in \mathcal{N}(t), \\
p_i(t)=0, \quad t<a_i \text{ or } t\ge d_i .
\end{align}
Let $\hat B_t$ be the forecast non-EV base load, $C_t$ be transformer capacity, and $\sigma_t$ be the standard deviation of base-load forecast error. A risk-aware capacity constraint is written as
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}(t)} p_i(t) + \hat B_t + \kappa\sigma_t \le C_t ,
\end{equation}
where $\kappa\ge0$ controls the safety buffer. A larger $\kappa$ leaves more capacity margin for uncertainty.

\section{Online Lyapunov-ADMM Scheduling}
\subsection{Deadline-Aware Virtual Queues}
The remaining demand of EV $i$ is modeled as a virtual queue:
\begin{equation}
Q_i(t+1)=\left[Q_i(t)-\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t\right]^+ .
\end{equation}
At arrival, $Q_i(a_i)=E_i$. To represent deadline urgency, define
\begin{equation}
w_i(t)=\frac{1}{d_i-t+\delta},
\end{equation}
where $\delta>0$ avoids division by zero. A vehicle with large remaining demand and few slots before departure receives a larger urgency weight.

\subsection{Per-Slot Lyapunov Problem}
The drift-plus-penalty idea leads to the following per-slot convex problem:
\begin{align}
\min_{\mathbf p(t)} \quad
& V\pi_t\sum_i p_i(t)\Delta t
-\sum_i w_i(t)Q_i(t)\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t \nonumber\\
&+V\beta\left(\sum_i p_i(t)-P_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)\right)^2
\end{align}
subject to the power and risk-buffered capacity constraints. The first term penalizes charging cost, the second term rewards serving urgent queues, and the third term smooths the aggregate charging load. The parameter $V$ controls the cost-delay trade-off.

A practical deadline safety floor is included. If an EV must receive a minimum charging rate in the current slot to remain feasible before departure, that rate is imposed as a lower bound. This avoids excessive delay when the soft queue term alone is insufficient.

\subsection{ADMM-Based Distributed Solver}
For each slot, the problem is written as
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf p,\mathbf z} \sum_i f_i(p_i;t)+g(\mathbf z;t), \quad \mathbf p-\mathbf z=0,
\end{equation}
where $f_i$ contains the EV-level price and urgency terms, while $g$ contains the load-smoothing term and the capacity constraint. The scaled ADMM updates are
\begin{align}
p_i^{k+1} &= \arg\min_{p_i\in\mathcal P_i} f_i(p_i;t)+\frac{\rho}{2}(p_i-z_i^k+u_i^k)^2,\\
\mathbf z^{k+1} &= \arg\min_{\mathbf z\in\mathcal Z} g(\mathbf z;t)+\frac{\rho}{2}\|\mathbf p^{k+1}-\mathbf z+\mathbf u^k\|_2^2,\\
\mathbf u^{k+1} &= \mathbf u^k+\mathbf p^{k+1}-\mathbf z^{k+1}.
\end{align}
The EV-side update uses local queue and deadline information, while the aggregator-side update enforces the transformer constraint and load-smoothing objective.

\subsection{Theoretical Properties}
\textbf{Proposition 1 (Per-slot convexity):} For a fixed active EV set and fixed forecasts at slot $t$, the per-slot problem is a convex program when $\beta\ge0$.

\emph{Proof:} The price and queue-service terms are linear in $\mathbf p(t)$. The smoothing term is a nonnegative quadratic function when $\beta\ge0$. The box constraints and the risk-buffered transformer constraint are affine. Hence the feasible set is convex and the objective is convex.

\textbf{Proposition 2 (Per-slot ADMM convergence):} If the split per-slot problem is feasible, $\rho>0$, and the EV-side and aggregator-side feasible sets are closed and convex, the two-block ADMM iterations converge to a primal-dual solution of the per-slot convex problem under the standard ADMM assumptions \cite{Boyd2011,Eckstein1992}.

ADMM is not introduced to change the optimization objective. Its role is implementation-oriented: EV-side updates use local queue states and charging limits, while the aggregator enforces the global transformer constraint. This separates local information from the coupled grid constraint and reduces the need for a fully centralized per-slot solve.

\textbf{Lyapunov interpretation:} The parameter $V$ follows the usual drift-plus-penalty interpretation \cite{Neely2010}: larger $V$ gives more weight to cost minimization, while smaller $V$ emphasizes queue service and deadline pressure. The analysis is used to guide tuning and to interpret the empirical cost-deadline trade-off; no new closed-form $O(1/V)$ or $O(V)$ bound is claimed.

The procedure is causal: it uses current EV states, current price, and current load forecast, but does not require future vehicle arrivals. This distinguishes it operationally from the offline centralized LP and offline ADMM baselines.

\section{Numerical Experiments}
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
The simulation uses a 24-hour horizon with 48 half-hour slots and a default fleet size of 50 EVs. The controlled sweeps vary transformer capacity, the Lyapunov parameter $V$, the risk-buffer parameter $\kappa$, random seeds, and fleet size. Additional tests use workplace-style and public-data profiles. The public-data profile uses real arrival times, departure times, and delivered energy from the 4TU/ElaadNL large office parking-lot dataset \cite{DeBont2025ElaadOffice}; electricity prices and base-load uncertainty are generated by the same evaluation framework. Metrics include charging cost, peak load, deadline violation, unserved energy, runtime, and capacity violation.

Six methods are compared: uncontrolled capped charging, greedy deadline-price charging, offline centralized linear programming (LP), dual decomposition, offline ADMM, and the proposed online Lyapunov-ADMM. The offline centralized LP is treated as an ideal lower-cost benchmark because it uses complete future information.

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} and Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoffs}(a) summarize the baseline comparison. Offline centralized LP and offline ADMM obtain the lowest cost, as expected under full future information. Online Lyapunov-ADMM is more expensive, but it reduces the average peak load from 186.34 kW to 163.28 kW relative to offline LP and removes capacity violations in this setting. The result supports a deployment-oriented interpretation of the online method rather than a cost-dominance claim.

The workplace-style sensitivity profile shows the same qualitative pattern: offline LP has lower cost, while online Lyapunov-ADMM lowers peak load and keeps capacity violation at zero. This case is used only to test scenario sensitivity.

\subsection{Public Charging-Data Check}
An external-validity check is conducted using five high-activity days from the 4TU/ElaadNL office parking-lot dataset. Each day uses up to 50 real sessions; start time, end time, and delivered energy are taken from the public CSV, while price and base-load uncertainty are generated by the same evaluation framework. With $V=0.8$ and $\kappa=1.0$, online Lyapunov-ADMM reduces mean peak load from 208.26 kW to 198.49 kW and lowers capacity violation from 3.8\% to 1.2\% relative to offline LP, but its deadline violation rate is 10.8\%. The result indicates that public office-parking sessions can be tighter than the synthetic setting and that $V$ and $\kappa$ must be tuned jointly.

\subsection{Capacity Pressure}
The capacity-pressure sweep examines whether the controller remains usable as transformer capacity tightens. Dual decomposition and greedy charging may leave nonzero unserved energy under tight capacity, while online Lyapunov-ADMM gives the most stable peak and capacity-safety behavior in the tested cases.

\subsection{Lyapunov Parameter and Risk Buffer}
Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoffs}(b) shows the effect of $V$. Small values emphasize deadline satisfaction, while large values give more weight to cost. In the tight-capacity case, $V=0.8$ to $1.2$ is a reasonable operating region; larger values reduce cost but create deadline violations.

The risk-buffer sweep evaluates $\kappa$ under aligned and inverted price-load relationships. Increasing $\kappa$ reduces capacity violation in both cases, but its cost impact depends on whether high-load periods coincide with high-price periods. The robust conclusion is safety improvement, not guaranteed cost reduction.

\subsection{Scalability}
The scalability sweep compares EV populations from 50 to 500 vehicles. Offline ADMM reaches about 33 seconds for 500 EVs in the implemented simulation. Online Lyapunov-ADMM remains fast because it solves the current per-slot problem rather than the full-day scheduling problem.

A larger fast scalability check for 1000 and 2000 EVs shows that centralized LP remains computationally efficient in these synthetic LP cases. Its limitation is not runtime alone, but its dependence on complete future information and global data. Online Lyapunov-ADMM runs in about 0.01 s in both large cases and keeps realized capacity violation at zero.

\section{Discussion}
The experiments indicate that the compared methods serve different operational purposes. Offline centralized LP and offline ADMM are cost-oriented benchmarks under full future information. Online Lyapunov-ADMM instead provides an online, distributed, and risk-aware trade-off with lower peak load and improved capacity safety.

The method has several limitations. The public-data check uses real charging-session times and energy demands, but the price and base-load uncertainty signals are simulated; it is therefore an external-validity check rather than a full field deployment. The risk model uses a simple safety margin instead of a full chance-constrained formulation. Battery degradation, vehicle-to-grid operation, renewable generation uncertainty, and asynchronous communication are not considered. These limitations define directions for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented an online risk-aware distributed EV charging framework that combines deadline-aware virtual queues, Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty control, and ADMM-based per-slot coordination. The method targets causal scheduling rather than full-information cost optimality. Numerical experiments show that it can reduce peak load and improve capacity safety, while the public-session check indicates that office-parking data requires careful tuning of cost, deadline, and risk parameters. Future work will extend the model to battery degradation, renewable uncertainty, and asynchronous distributed implementation.
