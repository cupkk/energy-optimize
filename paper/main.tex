\begin{abstract}
Large-scale electric vehicle (EV) charging networks require real-time coordination under uncertain vehicle arrivals, heterogeneous departure deadlines, time-varying electricity prices, and transformer capacity limits. Offline centralized optimization can obtain low charging cost when complete future information is available, but it is not directly causal and requires global information. This paper develops an online risk-aware distributed EV charging framework based on deadline-aware virtual queues, Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty control, and ADMM-based per-slot coordination. The remaining charging demand of each EV is modeled as a dynamic queue, and a risk buffer protects the transformer capacity constraint against base-load prediction errors. A drift upper bound is used to derive a tractable per-slot objective, while ADMM separates EV-side local updates from aggregator-side capacity coordination. Numerical experiments compare immediate capped charging, greedy charging, offline centralized optimization, dual decomposition, offline ADMM, and the proposed online Lyapunov-ADMM method. The results show that the proposed method improves peak-load and capacity-safety metrics in tight-capacity cases at the cost of higher charging cost than full-information offline benchmarks. A public-data check based on office-parking charging sessions further indicates that cost, deadline, and risk parameters must be tuned jointly.
\end{abstract}

Electric vehicle charging, online optimization, Lyapunov optimization, ADMM, distributed scheduling, risk-aware control.

\section{Introduction}
Coordinated EV charging is becoming an important problem in smart grids because uncoordinated charging can increase electricity cost, create new peak loads, stress distribution transformers, and complicate vehicle-grid interaction \cite{Clement2010,Deilami2011,Guille2009,Richardson2013,Yilmaz2012}. The scheduling problem is difficult because EVs have heterogeneous arrival times, departure deadlines, energy demands, and charging-rate limits. In addition, electricity prices and non-EV base load vary over time, so a charging policy must balance cost saving, peak-load reduction, user satisfaction, and transformer safety.

Many EV charging studies formulate the problem as an offline optimization task, where all vehicle information is known before scheduling. Such formulations are useful benchmarks, but practical charging stations often operate online: vehicles arrive randomly, remaining demand changes over time, and the aggregator must decide before future arrivals are known. The central question is therefore how an EV charging network can make scalable and risk-aware online decisions while preserving deadline satisfaction.

This paper combines Lyapunov online control with ADMM-based distributed coordination. Lyapunov control converts long-term deadline pressure into a sequence of tractable per-slot optimization problems. ADMM decomposes each per-slot problem into EV-side local updates and aggregator-side coordination. This design keeps the controller causal while retaining the distributed structure emphasized in decomposition-based EV charging methods.

The contributions are as follows. First, an online EV charging model is formulated with deadline-aware virtual queues, time-varying prices, and risk-buffered transformer constraints. Second, the Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty step is derived explicitly, including the role of urgency weights and the deadline safety floor. Third, a distributed ADMM implementation is developed for the resulting per-slot convex problem. Fourth, the method is evaluated against uncontrolled, greedy, centralized offline, dual-decomposition, and offline ADMM baselines using multi-seed synthetic experiments, parameter sweeps, ablations, scalability checks, and a public charging-session check.

\section{Related Work}
Centralized EV charging optimization has been widely studied for valley filling, cost minimization, grid constraints, and regulation services. Sortomme and El-Sharkawi developed optimal charging strategies for unidirectional vehicle-to-grid services \cite{Sortomme2011}. Sundstrom and Binding considered flexible charging with distribution-grid constraints \cite{Sundstrom2012}. Charging and discharging schedules, battery-health-aware charging, renewable-aware charging, and real-time charging-park management have also been studied in broader EV-smart-grid settings \cite{He2012,Bashash2010,Mwasilu2014,Zakariazadeh2014}. These methods provide useful cost benchmarks, but they typically require a central aggregator and sufficient information about the charging population.

Decentralized and distributed EV charging control has received significant attention. Gan, Topcu, and Low proposed decentralized and schedule-constrained charging protocols that exploit EV flexibility for valley filling \cite{Gan2013,Gan2013Allerton}. Ma, Callaway, and Hiskens studied decentralized charging control for large plug-in EV populations using game-theoretic ideas \cite{Ma2013}. Other online or heuristic methods address practical charging constraints and discrete charging rates \cite{Alonso2014,Masoum2015,Lee2021Discrete}. These studies show that distributed coordination can reduce central computation and communication burden, but many formulations remain deterministic or assume knowledge of the scheduling horizon.

ADMM is a standard tool for distributed convex optimization. Boyd et al. provide a general treatment of ADMM and explain why it is well suited to decomposable large-scale optimization problems \cite{Boyd2011}. The method is connected to Douglas--Rachford splitting, augmented Lagrangian decomposition, proximal algorithms, and multi-agent optimization \cite{Eckstein1992,Gabay1976,Parikh2014,Nedic2009}. ADMM has also been used in distributed optimal power flow \cite{DallAnese2013,Erseghe2014}. In EV charging, ADMM-style methods are attractive because EV-level objectives are local while transformer-capacity constraints are globally coupled.

Online stochastic optimization is another relevant line of work. Queue-based MaxWeight ideas provide the foundation for throughput-aware online scheduling \cite{Tassiulas1992,Tassiulas1993,Georgiadis2006,Stolyar2004}. Neely's Lyapunov optimization framework shows how drift-plus-penalty methods can make online decisions in stochastic systems without requiring future information \cite{Neely2010}. Risk-aware capacity management is related to robust, stochastic, and chance-constrained optimization \cite{Bertsimas2004,Bertsimas2011,BenTal2009,Shapiro2014,Nemirovski2006,Calafiore2006,Campi2008}. Reinforcement learning provides another online-control direction \cite{Sutton2018,Watkins1992,Mnih2015}; this paper instead uses a convex queue-based controller for interpretability and tunability.

Empirical charging datasets are increasingly important for evaluating charging algorithms. ACN-Data provides an open workplace and public charging dataset \cite{Lee2019}. Recent office-building charging studies analyze information value, predictive charging, parking-lot allocation, and user-facing guarantees \cite{Winschermann2023Value,Hoogsteen2023Robust,Arias2023Parking,Winschermann2023Guarantees}. This study uses synthetic experiments for controlled sweeps and performs a public-data check with the 4TU/ElaadNL large office parking-lot dataset \cite{DeBont2025ElaadOffice}. The intended gap is the combination of online stochastic operation, deadline-aware queue pressure, risk-aware capacity margins, and ADMM-based distributed coordination in one evaluation framework.

\section{System Model and Benchmarks}
\subsection{Charging Model}
Time is divided into $T$ slots of length $\Delta t$. Let $\mathcal{N}(t)$ be the set of active EVs at slot $t$. Each EV $i$ has arrival time $a_i$, departure time $d_i$, energy demand $E_i$, maximum charging power $p_i^{\max}$, and charging efficiency $\eta_i$. The decision variable $p_i(t)$ denotes the charging power assigned to EV $i$ at slot $t$.

The basic charging constraints are
\begin{align}
0 \le p_i(t) \le p_i^{\max}, \quad i\in\mathcal{N}(t), \label{eq:box}\\
p_i(t)=0, \quad t<a_i \ \text{or}\ t\ge d_i . \label{eq:availability}
\end{align}
Let $\hat B_t$ be the forecast non-EV base load, $B_t$ be the realized base load, $C_t$ be transformer capacity, and $\sigma_t$ be the standard deviation of the base-load forecast error. The online controller uses the risk-buffered capacity constraint
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}(t)} p_i(t)+\hat B_t+\kappa\sigma_t \le C_t ,
\label{eq:risk_capacity}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa\ge0$ controls the safety margin. A larger $\kappa$ leaves more capacity for load forecast errors.
For compact notation, define the risk-buffered EV charging margin as
\begin{equation}
\bar C_t=\left[C_t-\hat B_t-\kappa\sigma_t\right]^+ .
\label{eq:risk_margin}
\end{equation}
The load-smoothing reference used in the online controller is
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)=\gamma \bar C_t ,
\label{eq:p_ref}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma\in(0,1]$ is a reference-margin ratio. The reported experiments use $\gamma=0.82$ unless otherwise stated.

\subsection{Offline Full-Information Benchmark}
The offline centralized LP benchmark uses all vehicle information over the horizon. It minimizes energy cost plus a high penalty for unserved energy:
\begin{align}
\min_{\{p_i(t)\},\{s_i\}} \quad
& \sum_{t=1}^{T}\pi_t\Delta t\sum_i p_i(t) + M\sum_i s_i \label{eq:offline_lp_obj}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& 0\le p_i(t)\le p_i^{\max}\mathbf{1}_{a_i\le t<d_i}, \label{eq:offline_box}\\
& \sum_i p_i(t)\le C_t-\hat B_t-\kappa\sigma_t,\quad \forall t, \label{eq:offline_cap}\\
& \sum_{t=a_i}^{d_i-1}\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t+s_i\ge E_i,\quad \forall i, \label{eq:offline_energy}\\
& s_i\ge0,\quad \forall i. \label{eq:offline_slack}
\end{align}
Here $M$ is a large penalty coefficient and $s_i$ is the unserved energy slack. This benchmark is cost-oriented and noncausal: it is useful for comparison but is not an online policy. The dual-decomposition and offline ADMM baselines solve related full-information decompositions; they are included to separate the value of offline information from the value of distributed implementation.

\section{Online Lyapunov-ADMM Scheduling}
\subsection{Virtual Queues and Deadline Urgency}
The remaining demand of EV $i$ is modeled as a virtual queue:
\begin{equation}
Q_i(t+1)=\left[Q_i(t)-\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t\right]^+ ,
\label{eq:queue_update}
\end{equation}
with $Q_i(a_i)=E_i$. Define the delivered energy in a slot as
\begin{equation}
\mu_i(t)=\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t .
\label{eq:service}
\end{equation}
To represent deadline urgency, use
\begin{equation}
w_i(t)=\frac{1}{d_i-t+\delta},
\label{eq:urgency}
\end{equation}
where $\delta>0$ avoids division by zero. Vehicles with large remaining demand and fewer slots before departure receive larger service weight.

A practical deadline safety floor is also imposed. Let the minimum power required to keep EV $i$ feasible be
\begin{equation}
\ell_i(t)=
\left[
\frac{Q_i(t)-\eta_i p_i^{\max}(d_i-t-1)\Delta t}
{\eta_i\Delta t}
\right]^+ .
\label{eq:deadline_floor}
\end{equation}
The online problem uses $p_i(t)\ge \min\{p_i^{\max},\ell_i(t)\}$ whenever the EV is active and capacity permits. This floor prevents the controller from postponing charging until the remaining time is insufficient. If the aggregate floor exceeds the risk-buffered margin, the floor vector is proportionally relaxed,
\begin{equation}
\tilde\ell_i(t)=\ell_i(t)\frac{\bar C_t}{\sum_j\ell_j(t)} ,
\quad \text{when } \sum_j\ell_j(t)>\bar C_t ,
\label{eq:floor_relax}
\end{equation}
and the remaining capacity is allocated by the ADMM projection. Such cases are capacity-stress events and may lead to unserved energy.

\subsection{Drift-Plus-Penalty Derivation}
For a fixed slot $t$, define a weighted queue Lyapunov surrogate
\begin{equation}
L_w(t)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}(t)} w_i(t)Q_i^2(t).
\label{eq:lyapunov}
\end{equation}
Using $([x]^+)^2\le x^2$ and treating $w_i(t)$ as fixed within the slot,
\begin{align}
Q_i^2(t+1)-Q_i^2(t)
&\le \mu_i^2(t)-2Q_i(t)\mu_i(t). \label{eq:queue_square}
\end{align}
The one-slot weighted drift therefore satisfies
\begin{align}
\Delta_w(t)
&=L_w(t+1)-L_w(t) \nonumber\\
&\le B_t-\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}(t)} w_i(t)Q_i(t)\mu_i(t), \label{eq:drift_bound}
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
B_t=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}(t)}w_i(t)\mu_i^2(t)
\end{equation}
is bounded because charging powers are bounded. Let the per-slot operational penalty be
\begin{align}
C_t^{\mathrm{op}}(\mathbf p)
&=\pi_t\Delta t\sum_i p_i(t) \nonumber\\
&\quad+\beta\left(\sum_i p_i(t)-P_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)\right)^2 .
\label{eq:slot_cost}
\end{align}
Dropping bounded and decision-independent terms, minimizing an upper bound of
\begin{equation}
\Delta_w(t)+V C_t^{\mathrm{op}}(\mathbf p)
\label{eq:dpp}
\end{equation}
leads to the per-slot problem
\begin{align}
\min_{\mathbf p(t)}\quad
& V\pi_t\Delta t\sum_i p_i(t)
-\sum_i w_i(t)Q_i(t)\eta_i p_i(t)\Delta t \nonumber\\
&+V\beta\left(\sum_i p_i(t)-P_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)\right)^2
+\frac{\alpha}{2}\sum_i p_i^2(t) \label{eq:online_problem}
\end{align}
subject to \eqref{eq:box}, \eqref{eq:risk_capacity}, and the deadline floor. The parameter $V$ controls the empirical cost-deadline trade-off. The small proximal regularizer $\alpha\ge0$ improves numerical conditioning of the EV-side update; the implementation uses $\alpha=0.02$.

\subsection{ADMM-Based Distributed Solver}
For each slot, write \eqref{eq:online_problem} as
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf p,\mathbf z} \sum_i f_i(p_i;t)+g(\mathbf z;t),
\quad \mathbf p-\mathbf z=0,
\label{eq:admm_split}
\end{equation}
where $f_i$ contains the EV-level price and urgency terms, while $g$ contains the smoothing term and the capacity constraint. The scaled ADMM updates are
\begin{align}
p_i^{k+1} &=
\arg\min_{p_i\in\mathcal P_i}
f_i(p_i;t)+\frac{\rho}{2}(p_i-z_i^k+u_i^k)^2, \label{eq:admm_p}\\
\mathbf z^{k+1} &=
\arg\min_{\mathbf z\in\mathcal Z}
g(\mathbf z;t)+\frac{\rho}{2}\|\mathbf p^{k+1}-\mathbf z+\mathbf u^k\|_2^2, \label{eq:admm_z}\\
\mathbf u^{k+1} &= \mathbf u^k+\mathbf p^{k+1}-\mathbf z^{k+1}. \label{eq:admm_u}
\end{align}
The EV-side update uses local queue and charging-limit information. The aggregator-side update enforces the transformer constraint and the load-smoothing term.

\subsection{Properties and Implementation}
\textbf{Proposition 1 (Per-slot convexity):}
For a fixed active EV set and fixed forecasts at slot $t$, the per-slot problem is convex when $\beta\ge0$.

\emph{Proof:}
The price and queue-service terms are linear in $\mathbf p(t)$. The smoothing and proximal terms are nonnegative quadratic functions when $\beta\ge0$ and $\alpha\ge0$. The box, lower-bound, and risk-buffered transformer constraints are affine. Hence the feasible set is convex and the objective is convex.

\textbf{Proposition 2 (Per-slot ADMM convergence):}
If the split per-slot problem is feasible, $\rho>0$, and the EV-side and aggregator-side feasible sets are closed and convex, the two-block ADMM iterations converge to a primal-dual solution under the standard ADMM assumptions \cite{Boyd2011,Eckstein1992}.

ADMM is not introduced to change the optimization objective. Its role is implementation-oriented: EV-side updates use local queue states and charging limits, while the aggregator enforces the global transformer constraint. The Lyapunov interpretation follows the usual drift-plus-penalty intuition \cite{Neely2010}; this paper uses it to guide tuning and does not claim a new closed-form $O(1/V)$ or $O(V)$ bound.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
The experiments report the following metrics. Charging cost is
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathrm{cost}}=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\pi_t\Delta t\sum_i p_i(t).
\label{eq:metric_cost}
\end{equation}
Total load and peak load are
\begin{align}
L_t^{\mathrm{tot}} &= B_t+\sum_i p_i(t), \label{eq:total_load}\\
P_{\mathrm{peak}} &= \max_t L_t^{\mathrm{tot}} . \label{eq:peak}
\end{align}
The unserved energy ratio is
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{uns}}=
\frac{\sum_i Q_i(d_i)}{\sum_i E_i}.
\label{eq:unserved}
\end{equation}
The practical deadline violation rate uses a small tolerance $\epsilon_Q$:
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{dl}}=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \mathbf{1}\{Q_i(d_i)>\epsilon_Q\}.
\label{eq:deadline_violation}
\end{equation}
The realized capacity violation rate is
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{cap}}=
\frac{1}{T}\sum_t
\mathbf{1}\left\{B_t+\sum_i p_i(t)>C_t\right\}.
\label{eq:capacity_violation}
\end{equation}
Worst-user satisfaction is
\begin{equation}
S_{\min}=\min_i\left(1-\frac{Q_i(d_i)}{E_i}\right).
\label{eq:worst_satisfaction}
\end{equation}
Runtime is measured on the local implementation and should be interpreted as an implementation-level indicator rather than a hardware-independent complexity bound.

\section{Numerical Experiments}
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
The simulation uses a 24-hour horizon with 48 half-hour slots and a default fleet size of 50 EVs. The controlled sweeps vary transformer capacity, the Lyapunov parameter $V$, the risk-buffer parameter $\kappa$, random seeds, and fleet size. Additional tests use workplace-style and public-data profiles. The public-data profile uses real arrival times, departure times, and delivered energy from the 4TU/ElaadNL large office parking-lot dataset \cite{DeBont2025ElaadOffice}; electricity prices and base-load uncertainty are generated by the same evaluation framework.

Six methods are compared: immediate capped charging, greedy deadline-price charging, offline centralized LP, dual decomposition, offline ADMM, and online Lyapunov-ADMM. The immediate capped baseline charges active EVs as soon as possible but scales the powers when the risk-buffered capacity would be exceeded; it is therefore not a deliberately capacity-violating uncontrolled policy. Since the finite-iteration dual-decomposition baseline can leave small capacity mismatches, a deterministic feasibility projection is applied after the raw decomposition schedule is produced. The before-repair infeasibility statistics are recorded in the experiment logs, and the repair makes the reported dual-decomposition baseline more favorable rather than weaker. \section{Discussion}
The experiments indicate that the compared methods serve different operational purposes. Offline centralized LP and offline ADMM are cost-oriented benchmarks under full future information. Online Lyapunov-ADMM instead provides an online, distributed, and risk-aware trade-off with lower peak load and improved capacity safety.

The method has several limitations. The public-data check uses real charging-session times and energy demands, but the price and base-load uncertainty signals are simulated; it is therefore an external-validity check rather than a full field deployment. The risk model uses a simple safety margin instead of a full chance-constrained formulation. Battery degradation, vehicle-to-grid operation, renewable generation uncertainty, and asynchronous communication are not considered. These limitations define directions for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented an online risk-aware distributed EV charging framework that combines deadline-aware virtual queues, Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty control, and ADMM-based per-slot coordination. The method targets causal scheduling rather than full-information cost optimality. The expanded experiments show that it can reduce peak load and improve capacity safety, while public-session checks indicate that office-parking data requires careful tuning of cost, deadline, and risk parameters. Future work will extend the model to battery degradation, renewable uncertainty, and asynchronous distributed implementation.
